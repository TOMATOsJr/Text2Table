RULE-BASED RELATION EXTRACTION

Overview
Rule-based relation extraction identifies relationships between entities using manually designed linguistic patterns. These patterns describe how a relationship appears in text. When the system finds a sentence that matches a pattern, it extracts a structured triple from it.

Goal
Convert sentences into structured triples:

(subject, relation, object)

Example sentence:
Leonard Shenoff Randle was born on February 12, 1949 and played for the Seattle Mariners.

Expected triples:
(Randle, birth_date, February 12 1949)
(Randle, played_for, Seattle Mariners)

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Text Preprocessing
Clean the input paragraph to remove noise such as special tokens, extra whitespace, and irregular punctuation. The cleaned text should contain normal readable sentences.

Example:
Leonard Shenoff Randle (born February 12, 1949) is a former Major League Baseball player.

Step 2: Sentence Segmentation
Split the paragraph into individual sentences because pattern rules are easier to apply at the sentence level.

Example:
Sentence 1: Leonard Shenoff Randle (born February 12, 1949) is a former Major League Baseball player.
Sentence 2: He played for the Seattle Mariners.

Step 3: Named Entity Recognition
Identify entities in the sentence. Common entity types include:

PERSON
DATE
LOCATION
ORGANIZATION
PROFESSION

Example output:
Leonard Shenoff Randle → PERSON
February 12, 1949 → DATE
Seattle Mariners → ORGANIZATION

These entities will become nodes in the knowledge graph.

Step 4: Define Relation Patterns
Create patterns that represent relationships commonly found in biography text.

Examples of useful relations:

Birth date patterns: <Person> was born on <Date> <Person> (born <Date>)
born on <Date>

Example extraction:
Sentence: Randle was born on February 12, 1949.
Triple: (Randle, birth_date, February 12 1949)

Occupation patterns: <Person> is a <Profession> <Person> is an <Profession> <Person> worked as a <Profession>

Example:
Sentence: Randle is a former baseball player.
Triple: (Randle, occupation, baseball player)

Team membership patterns: <Person> played for <Organization> <Person> joined <Organization> <Person> was a member of <Organization>

Example:
Sentence: He played for the Seattle Mariners.
Triple: (Randle, played_for, Seattle Mariners)

Step 5: Pattern Matching
The system scans each sentence and checks whether it matches any predefined pattern.

If a pattern matches, the relation is identified and the corresponding entities are extracted.

Step 6: Triple Generation
For each detected relation, generate a triple:

(subject, relation, object)

Example output:
(Randle, birth_date, February 12 1949)
(Randle, occupation, baseball player)
(Randle, played_for, Seattle Mariners)

These triples can then be inserted into the knowledge graph.

Advantages
Rule-based extraction is simple to implement and easy to understand. It works well for repetitive sentence structures commonly found in biography text.

Limitations
Natural language varies greatly, and many sentences express the same fact in different ways. Because rules must be written manually, covering all possible variations becomes difficult. As a result, rule-based systems often miss many valid relations.
